\section{Experimental Results and Perspectives}

\subsection{Protocol and admissibility fix}

The experiment targets the 50\,\% human-driver regime on a ring road with $N{=}22$ vehicles ($L{=}300$\,m). We compare three methods: the rule-based mesoscopic adaptation alone (\textbf{No~RL}), and two residual RL policies (\textbf{PPO}, \textbf{SAC}) trained on top of it. Each episode starts from random initial conditions, and a velocity perturbation ($\Delta v = {-}3$\,m/s) is applied at $t{=}12$\,s after a 10\,s warm-up. The primary metric is the \emph{string-stability amplification ratio}: the maximum downstream-to-source speed-error ratio, where a value below~1 indicates disturbance attenuation.

A critical protocol bug was found and fixed before this campaign. The random initialization computed minimum inter-vehicle spacing without accounting for vehicle length ($\ell{=}5$\,m), producing net bumper-to-bumper gaps as low as 0\,m. Combined with a warm-up that also capped braking, earlier runs suffered collision-clamp events \emph{before} the perturbation, invalidating string-stability. The fix added vehicle length to the spacing bound, clamped initial closing rates to be kinematically safe, and allowed unrestricted braking during warm-up. In the saved training and evaluation runs used for this report, the pre-perturbation clamp count is zero throughout.

\subsection{Results}

Both RL methods early-stopped quickly (PPO at episode~15, SAC at~21), with best checkpoints at episodes~4 and~6. In practice, most of the useful learning happened almost immediately. Given the limited residual authority ($|\Delta\alpha| \leq 0.1$), this is not surprising: the policy can refine the baseline, but it cannot move very far away from it. Zero collision clamps were observed throughout training, which is consistent with the corrected initialization.

\subsection{Interpretation}

\textbf{The mesoscopic baseline is already strong.} The corrected no-RL run is already string stable, with an amplification ratio of~0.873. This matters because it shows the hand-designed architecture is doing real work on its own, rather than serving only as a scaffold for RL.

\textbf{RL adds only a modest gain in this regime.} SAC essentially matches the baseline on the primary metric while reducing late-regime speed variance by about 7\,\% and slightly increasing the minimum gap. PPO is a little worse than the baseline. With only ${\pm}10\,\%$ headway modulation, neither algorithm can move far from the rule-based policy.

\textbf{SAC is the more convincing residual learner.} Of the two RL methods, SAC is the only one that consistently improves the secondary metrics. That makes it the better candidate for future runs, even if the current gain is still small.

\textbf{The main result is methodological as much as numerical.} The comparison is now admissible. That matters because earlier runs were contaminated by pre-perturbation clamps, so their string-stability scores could not be trusted.

\subsection{Future work}

The next priority is \textbf{multi-seed evaluation} (10--20 seeds) to separate genuine method differences from seed artefacts. After that, \textbf{increasing the RL authority} ($|\Delta\alpha|$ from 0.1 to 0.2--0.3) would give the policy more room to differentiate from the baseline. The campaign should also be extended to \textbf{25\,\% and 75\,\% penetration rates}, and training should move to the \textbf{GPU-vectorized backend} to enable longer runs with larger batch sizes.
