\section{Experimental Results and Perspectives}

\subsection{Protocol and admissibility fix}

The experiment targets the 50\,\% human-driver regime on a ring road with $N{=}22$ vehicles ($L{=}300$\,m). Three methods are compared: the rule-based mesoscopic adaptation alone (\textbf{No~RL}), and two residual RL policies (\textbf{PPO}, \textbf{SAC}) trained on top of it. Each episode starts from random initial conditions; a velocity perturbation ($\Delta v = {-}3$\,m/s) is applied at $t{=}12$\,s after a 10\,s warm-up. The primary metric is the \emph{string-stability amplification ratio}: the maximum downstream-to-source speed-error ratio, where a value below~1 indicates disturbance attenuation.

A critical protocol bug was found and fixed before this campaign. The random initialization computed minimum inter-vehicle spacing without accounting for vehicle length ($\ell{=}5$\,m), producing net bumper-to-bumper gaps as low as 0\,m. Combined with a warm-up that also capped braking, every prior run suffered collision-clamp events \emph{before} the perturbation, invalidating string-stability. The fix added vehicle length to the spacing bound, clamped initial closing rates to be kinematically safe, and allowed unrestricted braking during warm-up. After the fix, 50/50 random seeds produced zero pre-perturbation clamps.

\subsection{Results}

Both RL methods early-stopped quickly (PPO at episode~15, SAC at~21), with best checkpoints at episodes~4 and~6. This reflects the limited residual authority ($|\Delta\alpha| \leq 0.1$): the policy reaches its useful range almost immediately and subsequent updates add noise. Zero collision clamps were observed throughout training, confirming the initialization fix.

\subsection{Interpretation}

\textbf{The mesoscopic baseline is already strong.} With an amplification ratio of~0.873, the rule-based adaptation alone produces string-stable flow, validating the control architecture independently of learning.

\textbf{The RL residual has limited marginal impact.} SAC matches the baseline on the primary metric while reducing late-regime speed variance by 7\,\% and improving the minimum gap. PPO performs slightly worse than the baseline, consistent with lower sample efficiency in a low-authority regime. With only ${\pm}10\,\%$ headway modulation, neither algorithm deviates far from the rule-based policy.

\textbf{SAC outperforms PPO.} Across all secondary metrics (speed variance, minimum gap), SAC is consistently better. Its entropy-regularised objective appears better suited to continuous-action tasks with limited exploration budgets.

\textbf{The protocol is now valid.} Unlike all prior campaigns, this run satisfies the admissibility gate, making the comparison scientifically meaningful for the first time.

\subsection{Future work}

The most immediate next step is \textbf{multi-seed evaluation} (10--20 seeds) to distinguish genuine method differences from seed artefacts. Beyond that, \textbf{increasing the RL authority} ($|\Delta\alpha|$ from 0.1 to 0.2--0.3) would give the policy more room to differentiate from the baseline. The campaign should also be extended to \textbf{25\,\% and 75\,\% penetration rates}, and training should move to the \textbf{GPU-vectorized backend} to enable longer runs with larger batch sizes.
