1       Introduction
1.1     A Short Description
The modernization of intelligent transportation systems (ITS) has necessitated a paradigm
shift from individual vehicle autonomy to cooperative automation. Vehicular platooning offers
a promising solution to traffic congestion, fuel inefficiency, and road safety.
    Thus, our work motivates an ”Adaptive Hybrid Control strategy” using RL to adjust the
weight between micro and macro. This transition from the standard Adaptive Cruise Con-
trol (ACC) to Cooperative Adaptive Cruise Control (CACC) introduces challenges in string
stability, the ability to attenuate disturbances, especially under communication delays.

1.2     Motivation
A fundamental trade-off exists in platoon control architectures:

     Local Control: Relies on local sensors (radar/LiDAR). It is robust to network failures
      and ensures collision avoidance but is prone to string instability in long platoons.

     Global Control: Relies on V2V data from the leader. It provides excellent string
      stability but is highly sensitive to communication delays and packet loss.

1.3     Our contributions
     Modeling mixed traffic with human-driven(HDVs) and connected autonomous vehicles
      (CAVs), improving capacity and stability, in addition to extending traffic analysis from
      open platoons to closed ring-road systems.

     Proposing a mesoscopic RL framework combining micro- and macro-level traffic states
      with incorporated resistance forces on our input to create the nearest real life simulation.

3     Reinforcement Learning
In this project, reinforcement learning (RL) is used to improve the behavior of connected and
automated vehicles (CAVs) in mixed traffic. The main idea is not to replace the baseline
controller, but to add an adaptive layer on top of it. This learned layer adjusts the time
headway policy according to the current traffic situation. In this way, the controller keeps its
physical interpretation while gaining the ability to react to complex traffic patterns that are
difficult to model explicitly.

3.1    Fundamentals
The RL problem is formulated as a Markov Decision Process (MDP),

                                         M = (S, A, P, r),

where S is the state space, A is the action space, P represents the traffic dynamics, and r is
the reward.
    At each time step, the agent observes the traffic state, selects an action, applies it to the
controller, and receives a reward. In the current implementation, the state of each CAV i is
defined as

    ref : efficiency term, encouraging the vehicle speed to remain close to the upstream mean

    rcf : comfort term, penalizing high jerk;

    rss : string stability term, penalizing amplification of spacing errors along the platoon;

    rσ2 : traffic smoothness term, penalizing large velocity variance in the traffic stream.

5     Experimental Results and Perspectives
5.1     Protocol and admissibility fix
The experiment targets the 50 % human-driver regime on a ring road with N =22 vehicles
(L=300 m). Three methods are compared: the rule-based mesoscopic adaptation alone (No RL),
and two residual RL policies (PPO, SAC) trained on top of it. Each episode starts from ran-
dom initial conditions; a velocity perturbation (∆v = −3 m/s) is applied at t=12 s after a
10 s warm-up. The primary metric is the string-stability amplification ratio: the maximum
downstream-to-source speed-error ratio, where a value below 1 indicates disturbance attenua-
tion.
    A critical protocol bug was found and fixed before this campaign. The random initialization
computed minimum inter-vehicle spacing without accounting for vehicle length (ℓ=5 m), pro-
ducing net bumper-to-bumper gaps as low as 0 m. Combined with a warm-up that also capped
braking, every prior run suffered collision-clamp events before the perturbation, invalidating
string-stability. The fix added vehicle length to the spacing bound, clamped initial closing
rates to be kinematically safe, and allowed unrestricted braking during warm-up. After the fix,
50/50 random seeds produced zero pre-perturbation clamps.

5.2     Results
Both RL methods early-stopped quickly (PPO at episode 15, SAC at 21), with best checkpoints
at episodes 4 and 6. This reflects the limited residual authority (|∆α| ≤ 0.1): the policy reaches
its useful range almost immediately and subsequent updates add noise. Zero collision clamps
were observed throughout training, confirming the initialization fix.

5.3     Interpretation
The mesoscopic baseline is already strong. With an amplification ratio of 0.873, the
rule-based adaptation alone produces string-stable flow, validating the control architecture
independently of learning.
    The RL residual has limited marginal impact. SAC matches the baseline on the
primary metric while reducing late-regime speed variance by 7 % and improving the minimum
gap. PPO performs slightly worse than the baseline, consistent with lower sample efficiency in
a low-authority regime. With only ±10 % headway modulation, neither algorithm deviates far
from the rule-based policy.
    SAC outperforms PPO. Across all secondary metrics (speed variance, minimum gap),
SAC is consistently better. Its entropy-regularised objective appears better suited to continuous-
action tasks with limited exploration budgets.
    The protocol is now valid. Unlike all prior campaigns, this run satisfies the admissibility
gate, making the comparison scientifically meaningful for the first time.

5.4    Future work
The most immediate next step is multi-seed evaluation (10–20 seeds) to distinguish genuine
method differences from seed artefacts. Beyond that, increasing the RL authority (|∆α|
from 0.1 to 0.2–0.3) would give the policy more room to differentiate from the baseline. The
campaign should also be extended to 25 % and 75 % penetration rates, and training should
move to the GPU-vectorized backend to enable longer runs with larger batch sizes.
